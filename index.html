About this map

Gulf Beds — Shellbed Atlas of South Australia

What is this map?

An interactive reference tool produced for Eyrelab, a registered South Australian environmental charity headquartered on the Eyre Peninsula and founded and directed by Emmanuel "Manny" Katz. Eyrelab's mission centres on three goals: repairing nature through science-led, community-driven shellfish reef restoration; empowering youth with hands-on marine science skills and field experience; and making change by connecting communities, Traditional Owners, researchers, and industry around shared restoration outcomes. This map is developed and maintained by Brian Arruda, researcher at Eyrelab, as a working research and communication tool — not a regulatory product. Eyrelab acknowledges the Nauo people as Traditional Owners of the waters this work takes place in.

How this map is organised

The layer panel is structured as a narrative, not a flat list. It moves through four stages that mirror the restoration research question — what was here, what's here now, what's being rebuilt, and what's coming back:

  1. Historical record — pre-collapse reef sites and species presence, drawn from the peer-reviewed literature.
  2. Modern presence — current field surveys and published research confirming where species exist today.
  3. Active restoration — reef-building programs currently underway across SA.
  4. Reef biodiversity recovery — community and citizen-science observations showing what's recolonising restored and remnant habitat.

Within stages 2 and 4, species are further split by role:

Tier 1 — reef-building species (Ostrea angasi, Malleus meridianus, Pinna dolabrata) are the structural focus of this research: the species whose reefs are being restored, and whose recruitment is the primary success metric. These appear throughout every stage of the map.

Tier 2 — associated reef biodiversity (Katelysia spp., Equichlamys bifrons) are not reef-builders themselves, but their presence is an indicator of reef recovery and habitat quality. They are shown at reduced visual prominence and are off by default, reflecting their supporting role in the evidence base rather than the project's core focus.

How to use this map

Layers button (top right) — toggle any layer. Each active layer opens an info panel on the left with sources, counts, and notes.

Field surveys layer — switch species using the lens row; status filters show/hide Present and Uncertain sites. Absent and not-surveyed sites show no icon.

+ Report sighting (top centre) — submit a community observation. Submissions are reviewed by Eyre Lab before being marked verified.

Basemap toggle — switch between Satellite and Ocean bathymetry views.

Reading the markers

Every O. angasi-related marker on this map uses the same gray shell icon (species = shape) across all layers — field surveys, restoration sites, research data, and community sightings. Other tracked species use their own distinct shell shapes. Context is layered on top of the shell via opacity and rings:

  • Full opacity — confirmed present / active site
  • 55% opacity — uncertain or indirect evidence (field surveys only)
  • No icon — absent or not yet surveyed
  • Gold halo ring — recruitment (juveniles/spat) confirmed
  • Solid / dashed teal ring — Sanctuary Zone vs Control site (Lindsey et al. research layer only)
  • Solid green / dashed amber ring — verified vs pending community sighting

In the Layers panel, a small dot marks each species by tier: dark teal for Tier 1 reef-builders, grey for Tier 2 associated species.

Historical reef markers (Martin et al. 2025) use coloured squares per species; a gold border marks a multi-species record. Gillies et al. 2018 harvest evidence uses colour-tiered triangles. A persistent legend sits at the bottom right of the map.

Field surveys — Eyre Lab, 2026

Sites assessed by Brian Arruda and Manny Katz, volunteering with Eyre Lab, in support of an independent researcher's oyster genetics work along the SA west coast. Surveys cover the Eyre Peninsula, Streaky Bay, Ceduna, and Whyalla regions, conducted in 2026.

Assessment methods: in-water (dive/snorkel, primary evidence), shore visual (intertidal only, does not confirm subtidal absence), secondhand (always recorded Uncertain). Status: Present requires in-water positive ID. Uncertain covers indirect evidence. Absent requires a negative in-water survey. No data means not yet assessed — distinct from Absent.

Research data — Lindsey et al. (2024 fieldwork, published 2026)

Lindsey, N., Connell, S.D., Katz, E. & McAfee, D. (2026). Community-based marine restoration to generate social licence and ecological knowledge for upscaling oyster reef restoration. People and Nature 8:301–315. doi:10.1002/pan3.70211

Co-author Emmanuel Katz (Eyre Lab) coordinated fieldwork with Dr Dominic McAfee. Eight Coffin Bay sites (4 Sanctuary Zones, 4 Controls) all confirmed O. angasi recruitment via shell-basket restoration units deployed Jan–Apr 2024. Fieldwork is dated 2024; the paper itself was published in 2026 — both years are noted in the layer panel to avoid ambiguity.

Eyrelab & the 25 Reefs Project

While surveying marine wildlife in Thorny Passage Marine Park, Eyrelab's team uncovered native oyster reefs buried under sediment — remnants of an ecosystem largely erased by nearly a century of colonial-era dredge fishing, which removed an estimated 1,500 km of shellfish reef habitat across South Australia. That discovery became Reviving Coffin Bay's Lost Oyster Reefs, a pilot project co-developed with University of Adelaide marine ecologists (Dr Sean Connell, Dr Dominic McAfee) and supported by a SA Department for Environment and Water Citizen Science Small Grant — the same fieldwork documented in the Lindsey et al. (2026) research layer on this map.

That pilot has since grown into 25 Reefs, a statewide restoration program led by Eyrelab: cleaned oyster shell, secured in biodegradable mesh, deployed in roughly 1-hectare units at sites with existing native oyster populations to capture spawning spat. Reefs are built largely through community working-bees and citizen-science volunteer effort, with first ecological results expected around 5 years post-deployment as oysters reach maturity.

This map also shows a separate, larger-scale restoration program funded by the SA Government in partnership with The Nature Conservancy, University of Adelaide, and local councils (Windara, Glenelg, O'Sullivan Beach, Nepean Bay) — distinct in scale and governance from Eyrelab's own 25 Reefs sites, though both pursue the same restoration goal. Coordinates for both programs are approximate centroids unless otherwise noted in the popup.

Community science

iNaturalist layer shows all research-grade O. angasi SA records — presence only, observer-effort biased toward Adelaide metro. Each marker links to the original observation. Community sightings are public submissions reviewed and verified by Eyre Lab; unverified entries should not be cited in academic work.

Taxonomy

All species use Latin binomials for consistency with the literature. The Brachidontes / Mytilus spp. field remains taxonomically unresolved pending voucher specimens.

Change & Disturbance layer

This layer compares historic species/reef records (Martin et al. 2025, Gillies et al. 2018) against modern data (2026 Eyre Lab field surveys, Lindsey et al. research data, iNaturalist research-grade observations) for the three Tier 1 reef-building species, within a 5km radius of each historic site — reflecting that historic leases often represented large reef patches rather than isolated point occurrences, and that many historic coordinates are themselves approximate. The radius extends to 8km where the historic site shares a named jetty, harbour, or wharf. Where a historic record sits near multiple distinctly-named modern survey localities (e.g. Coffin Bay's Eely Point, Yangie Bay, Kellidie Bay, Dutton Bay), the radius is capped at 1.5km unless the place names explicitly match, to avoid conflating these documented distinct sub-habitats with one another.

Markers use five states, distinguished by colour and fill style; click any marker for the specific case. Green, filled — positive presence signal, either consistent (historic record corroborated by modern data) or emergent (no historic baseline, but confirmed present today — computed only for modern Tier 1 field/research sites, not raw citizen-science points, to avoid over-claiming new occurrences from sparse historic coverage alone). Yellow, filled — indirect/uncertain modern evidence, or directly conflicting evidence (a negative field survey alongside a positive record nearby). Red, filled — confirmed loss: a historic record with an actual negative in-water survey nearby and no conflicting positive evidence. This is reserved for genuine disconfirming evidence, not absence of data. Gray, hollow outline (no fill) — unknown: no modern survey of any kind was found nearby. This is the largest category by far, since most of the SA coastline has not been recently surveyed; it represents a data gap, not a finding, and is rendered without colour fill specifically so it cannot be mistaken for a result.

Limitations

  • Survey coverage is opportunistic, not systematic — absence from the map does not mean absence in the water.
  • Field and community sighting data is stored in browser local storage; clearing cache deletes unsaved entries.
  • Historical coordinates (Martin, Gillies) are digitised approximations, not surveyed positions.
  • Gillies harvest data does not differentiate O. angasi from S. glomerata.
  • The seabed sediment layer is clipped to the SA bounding box, not the precise coastline.
  • The Change & Disturbance layer reflects survey coverage gaps as much as ecological change — see that layer's notes above before drawing conclusions about decline.
